Her History

The William G. Mather is a Great Lakes steamship freighter. It originially launched on May 23, 1925 and became out of service in 1980. Today, you can find the William G. Mather in Cleveland, Ohio as a museum. You can take tours on the ship and see what it was like to work and live on a freighter back in the 20s to 70s.

The William G. Mather was built in 1925 by Great Lakes Engineering Works, Ecorse, Michigan. She was the flagship of the Cleveland-Cliffs Iron Company. The William G. Mather got her name from the president of the Cleveland-Cliffs Iron Company at the time, William Gwinn Mather. The William G. Mather remained the company's flagship for 26 years. In 1951-52, the Edward B. Greene (now known as Kaye E. Barker of the Interlake Steamship Company) was built and became Cliffs' new flagship. The William G. Mather stayed an active part of the fleet until the end of the 1980 navigation season.

One of her missions was to supply the Allied Forces with steel they needed during World War II. To do this, the William G. Mather led a convoy of 13 freighters in 1941 through an ice-filled Upper Great Lakes to Duluth, Minnesota. This set a record for the first arrival in a northern port. This effort was featuered in the April 28, 1941 issue of Life magazine. She was also the first commercial vessel of the Great Lakes to be equipped with radar in 1946. The William G. Mather was also the first American Vessel to have an automated boiler system that was manufactured by Bailey Controls of Cleveland, Ohio.

Cleveland-Cliffs sold its two remaining operating ships to Rogue Steel Company in 1985. Gradually, they sold off their remaining idle vessels until the William G. Mather was the only remaining ship. On December 10, 1987, Cleveland-Cliffs donated the steamer William G. Mather to the Great Lakes Historical Society. The Historical Society was tasked to restore and preserve the ship and turn it into a floating maritime museum. Restoration began in October 1988 with the funding from local coundations, corporations, and individuals. There was fire damage to the galley and many cabins required a major restoration effort. Most of the work was supplied by volunteers who did everything to restore the William G. Mather to its former beauty. She was opened and moved to her permanant location on East Ninth Street Pier in Cleveland, Ohio on October 1990.

The Great Lakes Historical Society gave up ownership of the ship in September 1994. There was tremendous support to keep the William G. Mather in Cleveland, so the Harbor Heritage Society was created. The Harbor Heritage Society then became the owners of the Mather. After 10 years of negotiations, the City of Cleveland, represented by Mayor Jane L. Campbell signed a 40-year lease on June 15, 2003, allowing the William G. Mather to stay at the East Ninth Street Pier.

The Mather was a ship with many firsts. Because of the 1954 installation of a signle marine boiler and steam turbine engine, and her 1964 installation of the Bailey 760 Boiler Control System and American Shipbuilding AmThrust dual propeller bow thruster, she was dedicated as an American Society of Mechanical Engineers National Historic Mechanical Engineering Landmark. The Mather did have a sister ship, the Joseph H. Frantz, but this ship was converted to diesel and was scrapped in 2005 after 80 years of continuous use.
